🎙 Your 15-Week Plan
This plan is built around one goal: getting you to the IKEA Criterium start line fit, fresh, and ready to race. Four progressive phases take you from building the habit of structured training all the way through to race sharpness. Each session has a specific purpose — nothing is filler. Easy days are genuinely easy so hard days can be genuinely hard. That contrast is where fitness is made. Trust the process, log your rides, and the results will follow.

Sleep & Hydration

Your Secret Weapons
💧 Your Back + Dehydration Connection
Your spinal discs are largely water. When dehydrated they compress more easily — which is why you can ride hard, feel fine, then twist awkwardly the next day and hurt your back. The bike didn't cause it. The dehydration did. Drink water every single day — not just on ride days.
🎙 Why These Two Things Matter More Than Any Single Workout
Here's the honest truth: you can do everything else perfectly and still underperform if you're chronically under-slept and dehydrated. At 36, your recovery hormones (testosterone, HGH) are already declining compared to your 20s. Sleep is when those hormones do their work — rebuilding muscle, burning fat, consolidating fitness gains. And dehydration of even 2% of body weight measurably reduces power output and makes every effort feel harder than it is. These aren't soft lifestyle suggestions — they're physiological requirements for your goal.

💤 Sleep

Target: 7–8 hours every night. Non-negotiable.

Ideal: 10:30 PM → 6:30 AM
  • Sleep is when fat loss actually happens. HGH (human growth hormone) peaks during deep sleep — it's your body's primary fat-burning and muscle-building hormone
  • Under 7 hours = higher cortisol. Elevated cortisol tells your body to store fat, especially belly fat — the opposite of your goal
  • Sleep debt kills training gains. One bad night reduces power output by 3–8% the next day. A week of short sleep can wipe out gains from a week of training
  • 36-year-olds need more recovery, not less. Your tissues repair more slowly than at 22 — sleep is how you compensate for that
  • Cravings are a sleep problem. Under-slept people eat 300–500 more calories per day on average due to ghrelin (hunger hormone) spikes
Your Sleep Playbook
9:30 PMScreens go to night mode or off. Blue light suppresses melatonin — your sleep trigger hormone. Even 30 min less screen time before bed meaningfully improves sleep quality.
10:00 PMWind-down begins. Dim lights. No more eating. You can read, stretch, or do the evening mobility routine. Your body temp drops to trigger sleep — cool room helps (65–68°F / 18–20°C).
10:30 PMIn bed. Consistent bedtime is more powerful than sleep duration — your circadian rhythm responds to schedule. Even on weekends, stay within 30 min of this time.
Post-RideHard evening rides (Tue/Fri) raise core temp and cortisol. If you ride after 7 PM, take 60 min to cool down before attempting sleep — a cool shower helps a lot here.
CaffeineCut caffeine after 1–2 PM. Caffeine's half-life is 5–6 hours — a 3 PM coffee still has half its stimulant effect at 8 PM. This is the single most common hidden cause of poor sleep quality.
AlcoholEven 1–2 drinks fragments deep sleep and REM, slashing recovery quality. If you do drink, finish 3+ hours before bed and hydrate aggressively.

💧 Hydration

Target: 3.0–3.5 L daily. More on training days.
Your Daily Water Target
Click drops to track your glasses (each = ~300ml). Goal: fill all 10+
  • 2% dehydration = measurable power loss. At 207 lbs, that's just 2 kg of water. Your ride will feel harder than it should for no training benefit
  • Desk workers are chronically underhydrated. Air conditioning, no movement cues, and forgetting to drink are the norm. You're probably starting most rides already behind
  • Thirst is a late signal. By the time you feel thirsty, you're already 1–2% dehydrated. Drink on schedule, not on demand
  • Hydration accelerates fat loss. Your kidneys need water to metabolize fat — dehydration literally slows fat burning
  • Electrolytes matter on training days. Plain water isn't enough on ride days — you lose sodium, potassium, and magnesium through sweat. Add electrolytes or eat salty food post-ride
When to Drink
Wake Up500 ml immediately. You've been fasting and not drinking for 7–8 hrs. This one habit alone is transformative.
Morning500 ml before or with breakfast. Add a pinch of salt + squeeze of lemon if you can — natural electrolytes.
At DeskKeep a 1L bottle visible on your desk. Drink it by noon, refill, drink it by 3 PM. Out of sight = out of mind.
Pre-Ride500 ml in the 2 hours before riding. Never start a ride feeling dry-mouthed.
During Ride1 full bottle (500–750 ml) per hour of riding. In summer heat, increase to 750 ml–1 L/hr. Sip constantly — don't wait until you're thirsty.
Post-Ride500–750 ml with electrolytes within 30 min. Continue drinking through evening. Urine should be pale yellow — dark means you're behind.

The Science of Sleep & Weight Loss

A landmark study found that people on a calorie deficit who slept 8.5 hrs lost 55% more body fat than those sleeping 5.5 hrs on the same deficit. The sleep-deprived group lost mostly muscle. At 36, with declining testosterone, this effect is even more pronounced. Your sleep isn't separate from your weight loss plan — it is part of the plan.

The Science of Hydration & Performance

Research consistently shows that losing 2% of body weight in sweat reduces aerobic power by 5–8% and increases perceived effort significantly. At 207 lbs, that's just 4 lbs of sweat. In a criterium, that's the difference between hanging on and getting dropped. Hydration is the cheapest, easiest performance upgrade you have.

Desk Worker Fixes
Daily mobility · 6 exercises · 10–15 min
›
Why this matters
8 hours sitting shortens your hip flexors, rounds your thoracic spine, and shuts off your glutes. Tight hip flexors alone can cost 10–15% of power output. 10 minutes daily undoes the damage.
1Hip Flexor Release60 sec each side›
How: Kneeling lunge — back knee on floor, drive hips forward gently. Hold and breathe. Progress to couch stretch with back foot elevated.
Why: Shortened from sitting. Causes lower back pain on the bike and shortens your pedal stroke.
2Glute Bridges3 × 15 reps›
How: Feet flat on floor, knees bent. Drive hips up, hold 2 sec at top, lower slowly. Squeeze glutes hard at the top.
Why: Glutes switch off from sitting. Your biggest cycling muscle goes dormant — activate it daily.
3Thoracic Spine Opening10 slow reps each›
How: Cat-Cow on hands and knees — inhale, drop belly and lift head (cow); exhale, round spine to ceiling (cat). Then Thread the Needle: thread arm under body, 8 reps each side.
Why: Rounded upper back compresses your lungs and causes neck pain on the bike.
4Chest & Shoulder Opener45 sec each side›
How: Doorway chest stretch — forearm on frame, lean through gently. Then Wall Angels: back flat against wall, slide arms up and down slowly. Go easy on the collarbone side.
Why: Pecs tighten from forward posture. Limits breathing and causes shoulder ache in riding position. Helps collarbone mobility from the old break.
5Seated Spinal Twist45 sec each side›
How: Sit on floor, legs straight. Bend one knee, place foot outside opposite thigh. Twist toward bent knee using elbow as leverage. Sit tall and breathe into the twist.
Why: Decompresses vertebrae, mobilizes thoracic spine, directly relieves lower back tension. One of the best things you can do for your back.
6Hamstring & Calf — Downward Dog30 sec, pedal heels slowly›
How: From hands and knees, tuck toes and lift hips up and back into an inverted V. Press heels toward the floor. Slowly pedal heels alternately. Hold and breathe.
Why: Stretches hamstrings, calves, and the entire back line simultaneously. Tight hamstrings reduce your pull-through phase. Tight calves cause foot numbness on longer rides.
7Tai Chi Closing Flow2–3 minutes›
How: Stand tall, feet shoulder-width. (1) Weight Shifting — slowly shift weight side to side, 8 times each. (2) Cloud Hands — slow horizontal arm circles across the body, 6 each direction. (3) Wave Hands — arms float up on inhale, press down on exhale, 8 reps. Move as slowly as you can. Let the breath lead.
Why: Resets the nervous system after desk work. Builds the body awareness that improves your position on the bike. The slow movement is deceptively powerful — don't rush it.

How: Stand tall. (1) Weight Shifting — shift slowly side to side, 8 times. (2) Brush Knee — slow forward step with opposing arm push, 6 each leg. (3) Cloud Hands — horizontal arm arcs with hip rotation, 6 each direction. Keep everything slow — this is priming, not training.
Why: Activates the hip stabilizers and shoulder girdle before loading them. Wakes up the nervous system gently. You'll feel the difference in your Bird-Dogs and planks when you start warm.
2Bird-Dogs10 reps each side›
How: Hands and knees. Extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously. Hold 2 sec at top. Slow and controlled. Keep hips level.
Why: Stabilizes spine and core. Directly strengthens lower back without loading it. One of the best back protection exercises.
3Dead Bugs10 reps each side›
How: Lie on back, arms to ceiling, knees bent at 90° in the air. Slowly lower opposite arm and leg toward floor without arching lower back. Return and repeat.
Why: Deep core stability without spinal compression. Protects lower back from the inside out.
4Glute Bridges3 × 15 reps›
How: Feet flat, knees bent. Drive hips up, hard squeeze at top for 2 sec. Lower slowly.
Why: Activates glutes that switch off from sitting. Direct carryover to cycling power.
5Clamshells3 × 12 each side›
How: Lie on side, hips stacked, knees bent at 45°. Keep feet together, rotate top knee up like a clamshell opening. Lower slowly. Don't let hips roll back.
Why: Activates gluteus medius — the hip stabilizer that switches off from sitting. Critical for lower back and knee health on the bike.
6The Plank60 seconds›
How: Forearms on floor, body straight from head to heels. Squeeze everything — glutes, abs, quads. Breathe steadily. Don't let hips sag.
Why: Builds endurance for your cycling posture. Strong plank = less back fatigue on the bike.
7Farmer's Walk45–60 seconds›
How: Pick up two equal weights. Stand tall, shoulders back and down, walk slowly and deliberately. Don't let the load pull you sideways.
Why: Builds grip, core stability, and shoulder resilience. Stops the shoulder sway that contributes to collarbone strain.
8Towel Pass-Throughs15 reps›
How: Hold a towel with both hands, arms straight in front. Slowly pass it up and over your head and down behind you. Reverse. Go only as far as comfortable.
Why: Keeps shoulder girdle mobile. Specifically helpful for collarbone stiffness from the old break.
9The Sphinx60–90 seconds›
How: Lie face down, prop on forearms, elbows under shoulders. Let lower back passively arch while pelvis stays on floor. Breathe and hold.
Why: Decompresses spine. Finishes the circuit by undoing all the flexion from the exercises and the day.

Full Strength & Mobility
Home workout · 11 exercises · 35–40 min · Great days
›
💪 Great day energy
Full home workout — no gym required. Bodyweight and simple household weights. Builds on the Bulletproof Circuit with progressions and harder variations. You'll earn this one.
1Bird-Dogs — Progressed3 × 12 each side›
How: Same movement but add a 3-second hold at full extension and draw slow circles with your extended hand. Keep hips absolutely level.
Why: More time under tension = more lower back and core strength.
2Dead Bugs — Progressed3 × 12 each side›
How: Add a 2-second pause at the bottom of each rep with lower back pressed firmly into the floor. Increase range of motion gradually.
Why: Deeper core activation. More protective strength for lower back.
3Single-Leg Glute Bridge3 × 10 each leg›
How: Standard bridge but extend one leg straight. Drive up with the planted leg only. Keep hips level — don't let extended side drop.
Why: Isolates each glute. Fixes imbalances that cause back and hip problems.
4Clamshells — Banded3 × 15 each side›
How: Same as standard clamshells but add a resistance band just above the knees. Keep movement slow and controlled. Feel the burn in the outer glute.
Why: Progressive overload on the gluteus medius. Builds hip stability that protects your lower back in every movement.
5Plank with Shoulder Taps3 × 45 sec›
How: In plank position, lift one hand and tap opposite shoulder. Replace. Alternate. Keep hips still — no rocking. This is the challenge.
Why: Anti-rotation core strength. Directly transfers to cycling stability and back protection.
6Farmer's Walk — Heavy3 × 60 seconds›
How: Load up as heavy as you can while maintaining perfect posture. Shoulders packed down. Core braced. Walk deliberately.
Why: Heavy carries build functional strength that protects the spine in real life — those "twist weird" moments that hurt your back.
7Towel Pass-Throughs3 × 15 reps›
How: Try narrowing your grip slightly each set. Always stay within a comfortable range, especially the collarbone side.
Why: Collarbone mobility. Shoulder health. Don't skip this.
8Romanian Deadlift3 × 10 reps›
How: Hold weights in front of thighs. Hinge at hips, pushing them back, keeping back straight and chest proud. Lower weights along legs until deep hamstring stretch. Drive hips forward to stand. Hip hinge — not a squat.
Why: Directly strengthens the posterior chain — hamstrings, glutes, lower back. The best exercise for back resilience.
9World's Greatest Stretch5 reps each side›
How: Step into deep lunge. Place same-side hand on floor inside front foot. Rotate opposite arm up toward ceiling, eyes follow. Return and repeat.
Why: Opens hips, thoracic spine, and shoulders all in one movement. Full body mobility in a single exercise.
10Seated Spinal Twist45 sec each side›
How: Sit on floor, legs straight. Bend one knee, foot outside opposite thigh. Twist toward bent knee, elbow as leverage. Sit tall and breathe.
Why: Decompresses the spine after all the strength work. Keeps your back mobile and healthy.
11Sphinx into Cobra60 sec sphinx + 10 slow cobras›
How: Start in sphinx (forearms). Hold 60 sec. Press up onto hands for cobra, arms straight. Lower slowly. Repeat 10 times with breath.
Why: Maximum spinal decompression. Finishes the session by completely undoing all the flexion of the day.
12Tai Chi Closing Form5–8 minutes›
How: Stand and transition from the floor work. (1) Rooting — stand still, eyes closed, 60 sec — just breathe and feel your body. (2) Brush Knee — 8 slow forward steps each side, moving through the hip not the back. (3) Cloud Hands — 10 slow arcs, full shoulder and thoracic rotation. (4) Wave Hands Like Clouds — arms rise and fall with breath, weight shifts side to side, 8 reps. (5) Standing Meditation — feet together, hands folded, 5 deep breaths. You're done.
Why: After 35–40 min of real work your system is activated. This deliberately closes the session — signals the body to switch from stress to recovery. The hip and thoracic rotation directly addresses the collarbone and lower back. You'll sleep better tonight because of this.

🌿 The Golden Rule
›
Never create your calorie deficit by under-fueling training. Eat before you ride. Eat after you ride. Create the deficit through your other meals. Your fat stores fuel rides — your muscles shouldn't have to. Cyclists who skip pre-ride meals lose muscle, not fat.

What to Prioritize
🥩 Lean Proteins
›
Chicken breast, salmon, tuna, cod, turkey, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, tofu, tempeh, whey protein. Hit 175g+ daily — this protects your muscle while your body burns fat.
🌾 Complex Carbs
›
Oats, quinoa, farro, brown rice, sweet potato, whole grain bread. Time these around your rides — eat them before and after training, scale back on rest days.
🥦 Vegetables & Fruits
›
Fill half your plate at every meal. Spinach, kale, broccoli, green beans, bell peppers, asparagus, cucumber, tomatoes. Fruits for snacks and pre-ride fuel — banana, berries, apple, strawberries.
🥑 Healthy Fats
›
Avocado, almond butter, olive oil, flaxseed, chia seeds, nuts. These support hormone function and joint health — essential at 36. Don't fear fat, just choose the right kind.
💧 Hydration First
›
Water is your primary drink. Electrolyte water especially around training. Herbal teas count fully. Black coffee counts ~50%. 3L daily minimum. Hungry? Drink water first.
⚡ Carb Timing
›
Use quick carbs (banana, toast, honey, energy gel) before and during long or intense rides. Use complex carbs for all other meals. On rest days, reduce carbs and increase protein.
Foods to Avoid
🚫 Ultra-Processed Foods
›
Packaged snacks, frozen pizzas, flavored chips, sugary cereals.
Why: High in added sugars, trans fats, and sodium — low in actual nutrients.
🥤 Sugary Drinks
›
Soda, sweetened iced tea, energy drinks, sports drinks (unless riding 90+ min).
Why: Liquid sugar causes blood sugar spikes and crashes with zero satiety.
🍟 Fried Foods
›
Fries, deep-fried chicken, battered foods.
Why: Inflammatory fats slow digestion and cause sluggishness on rides.
🍺 Excess Alcohol
›
Especially beer and sugary cocktails.
Why: Slows recovery, adds empty calories, dehydrates, and fragments your sleep — killing the HGH release that burns fat overnight.
🍞 White Refined Carbs
›
White bread, pastries, donuts — occasional is okay, excess is the problem.
Why: Low fiber means blood sugar swings and more cravings.
🥣 Heavy Sauces & Dressings
›
Alfredo sauce, ranch, creamy dressings.
Why: High in calories with minimal nutritional payoff. Swap for olive oil, lemon, or balsamic.
🌭 Low-Quality Proteins
›
Processed deli meats, sausages, hot dogs.
Why: High in saturated fat, sodium, and preservatives.

⚠️ The Z3 Trap
Most amateur cyclists spend too much time in Zone 3 — it feels productive but it's the worst zone for adaptation. It's too hard to allow recovery, and too easy to build fitness. Your Thursday ride must stay in Z1–Z2. If it drifts to Z3, slow down. The ability to ride truly easy is a skill, and it takes discipline.

The Science
🔥 How Fat Loss Actually Works
›
1 lb of fat = ~3,500 calories. A ~500 kcal/day deficit = ~1 lb/week. That's ~300 kcal from eating smarter + ~200 kcal from increased training. Every 10 lbs lost = 1.5–2% faster on accelerations. You're not just losing weight — you're gaining speed.
📊 Tracking Progress
›
Weigh yourself weekly, not daily. Daily weight swings 1–3 lbs from water, food, and hormones — it means nothing. Weekly trends are what matter. Same day, same time, before eating. Log it here.

Also track: energy levels on rides, how your kit fits, breathing ease, resting heart rate. These often show progress before the scale does.
✅ Signs You're on Track
›
• Easier breathing during rides
• Increased endurance or faster times
• Better sleep quality
• Clothing fits looser
• Lower resting heart rate
• Less fatigue on easy days

Any three of these = the plan is working. Trust the process.

Criterium Race Skills

Practice These Every Week
What a Criterium Actually Is
A criterium is a short circuit race — typically a 1–2 km loop raced repeatedly for 45–60 min. It's fast, technically demanding, and tactical. The key skills: cornering at speed, holding a wheel in a pack, surviving repeated accelerations, and knowing when to go for the sprint. As a first-timer, your goal is to finish safely, learn the dynamics, and have fun. Everything else is a bonus.
🔄
Cornering
  • Brake before the corner, never in it
  • Outside foot at 6 o'clock at the apex — weight through that pedal
  • Look through the turn to the exit, not at the apex
  • Accelerate out of the corner — don't coast out
  • Practice weekly in a quiet parking lot. Do 20+ corners every session
🌬️
Drafting
  • Drafting at 30 km/h saves 25–30% energy — it's massive
  • Sit 1–1.5 bike lengths behind the wheel ahead
  • Slightly offset in crosswind — off their hip, not directly behind
  • Never overlap wheels — if they swerve, you go down
  • Find any group ride before race day to practice this
⚡
Accelerating Out of Corners
  • The "accordion effect" — gaps open in corners, back of pack must sprint every lap
  • Staying in the front 30% of the pack eliminates this problem
  • Spin your gear up before accelerating — don't mash from slow speed
  • Your Friday VO₂ session trains exactly this: surge, recover, repeat
🧠
Race Strategy — First Timer
  • Goal #1: Finish safely. That is a full win for race one
  • Start mid-pack. Don't fight for the front in the first lap
  • If dropped, don't panic — settle into your pace and time trial it
  • Ride the course beforehand — count corners, find technical sections
  • Count laps from the start. Know when the bell lap is
  • Save your sprint for the final 2 laps — don't blow up early
🎯
Cadence & Gearing
  • Target 90–100 RPM in the race — higher cadence = less muscle fatigue
  • Most beginners pedal at 70–75 RPM — too slow for criterium dynamics
  • Practice high cadence (95+ RPM) on every easy ride
  • Into corners: shift down 1–2 gears so you can accelerate out immediately
💬
Pre-Race Prep
  • Ride the circuit the week before — know every corner by name
  • Talk to experienced riders at the start line — most are happy to help
  • Warm up properly — cold legs in a fast crit is dangerous
  • Pin your number securely — a flapping number is a distraction
  • Check your brakes, tires, and shifting the night before. Not the morning of
